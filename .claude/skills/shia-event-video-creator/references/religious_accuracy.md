# Religious Accuracy Guardrails

Content for Al-Thaqalayn audiences is held to a high standard. Small errors of doctrine or history damage trust. Use this file to self-check before emitting prompts.

## Depicting sacred figures

**The core rule:** Faces of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the twelve Imams (عليهم السلام), the Prophets (عليهم السلام), and Lady Fatima al-Zahra (سلام الله عليها) are never depicted. Their faces are always obscured by radiant light.

This is the Shia convention and matches the user's Al-Thaqalayn content. Do not debate this rule or offer to generate "just for a test" without the halo. If the user ever asks for faces visible (they likely won't — their existing content confirms this), treat it as a mistake or a test and hold the line.

**Who exactly is covered:**
- Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — always veiled
- The Twelve Imams: Ali, Hasan, Husayn, Ali Zayn al-Abidin, Muhammad al-Baqir, Ja'far al-Sadiq, Musa al-Kazim, Ali al-Rida, Muhammad al-Jawad, Ali al-Hadi, Hasan al-Askari, Muhammad al-Mahdi — all veiled
- Fatima al-Zahra (س) — always veiled
- Other daughters of the Prophet, wives of the Prophet (Ummahat al-Mu'minin), other Prophets (Isa, Musa, Ibrahim, etc.) — veiled
- Angels (Jibril, etc.) — not depicted as figures; suggested only through light shafts

**Who may have visible faces (but kept indistinct):**
- Sahaba (Companions) not from Ahl al-Bayt — faces okay, but rendered as painterly crowd, not individually characterized
- Historical enemies (Yazid, Ibn Ziyad, Shimr, Muawiyah, Abu Sufyan, the Quraysh assassins) — may be shown, often in shadow; do not humanize with sympathetic close-ups
- Ordinary bystanders, crowds, soldiers — freely shown but crowd-style

## Honorifics in generated text

Every time these names appear in captions, prompts, or overlays:

- **Prophet Muhammad** → always followed by **ﷺ** (or spelled out "peace be upon him and his family") or the Shia salawat "صلى الله عليه وآله"
- **Imams (Ali through Mahdi)** → followed by **عليه السلام** or **(ع)** or "peace be upon him"
- **Fatima** → **سلام الله عليها** or **(س)** or "peace be upon her"
- **Sahaba** (Abu Talib, Hamza, Bilal, Salman, Abu Dharr, Miqdad, Ammar, Malik al-Ashtar, etc. — those revered in Shia tradition) → **رضي الله عنه** or "may Allah be pleased with him"

Do not add honorifics to figures Shia tradition treats as adversarial (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman — this is sensitive; in neutral historical description, just use the name without honorifics, which is the Shia convention).

## Doctrinal positions reflected in scene choices

The following are Shia positions that should come through in how events are framed:

1. **Ghadeer is an explicit appointment of Ali ع as the successor (wali/mawla).** Not a vague declaration of brotherhood. Scene 4 (the raising of the hand) and scene 5 (the declaration) must communicate succession.
2. **The Prophet did not die of natural causes alone** — many Shia narrations indicate poisoning. Don't depict this in a Prophet's death scene; simply frame as "the Prophet's ﷺ passing" without speculation.
3. **Fadak was unjustly confiscated from Fatima س.** Her khutbah in the mosque (the Fadakiyya) is a rightful protest.
4. **The attack on Fatima's س house happened** — the burnt door, the broken rib, the miscarriage of Muhsin. Depict with dignity (see events.md — suggest, don't show).
5. **Karbala was a divinely-destined stand, not a political miscalculation.** Imam Husayn ع chose martyrdom to preserve Islam. The framing should be of conscious sacrifice, not tragedy-as-accident.
6. **Imam al-Mahdi ع is alive in occultation.** If depicting him, show him always from behind or distance, veiled in light like the others, never identified with a specific age or face.

## Sources to lean on

Authentic Shia sources for narrative beats:

- **Nahj al-Balagha** (Imam Ali's ع sermons, letters, sayings)
- **al-Kafi** by al-Kulayni (hadith)
- **Bihar al-Anwar** by al-Majlisi (comprehensive hadith compilation)
- **al-Irshad** by al-Mufid (biographies of the 12 Imams)
- **Kitab Sulaym ibn Qays** (early Shia history, especially Saqifa and aftermath)
- **Maqtal al-Husayn** by al-Khwarizmi (detailed Karbala narrative)
- **Lahoof** (Luhuf) by Sayyid ibn Tawus (Karbala)
- **Tarikh al-Ya'qubi** (early history with Shia sympathies)
- **Tafsir al-Burhan** / **Tafsir al-Safi** (Shia Quranic commentaries)

When the user asks for an event's narrative, these are the sources to cite if citation is needed. Do not invent specific narrations; if uncertain, describe the general narrative without attributing a specific source.

## Things to avoid

- **Sectarian attack framing.** Even when depicting events where Sunni and Shia narratives diverge (Saqifa, the attack on Fatima's س house, Siffin, Jamal), frame the Shia position dignifiedly rather than as "proof" content. The audience is already Shia; they want reverence, not debate.
- **Speculation about physical appearance.** The tradition is specifically silent on the face for a reason — do not describe eye color, skin tone, specific features of sacred figures even in prompts the user won't see. The halo handles it.
- **Mixing events.** Ghadeer is not Arafat. Mubahala is not Tabuk. Be precise.
- **Inventing dialogue.** If a specific line is attributed to a sacred figure, it should be sourced. When in doubt, use well-known hadith (Hadith al-Thaqalayn, Hadith al-Manzilah, Hadith al-Kisa, the Ghadeer declaration).
- **Modern political imagery.** Resistance Axis flags, modern Iranian/Lebanese/Iraqi political symbols — keep them out. The videos should feel timeless and devotional, not geopolitical.

## When something is doctrinally uncertain

If the user asks you to depict an event with disputed details (e.g., the number of people present at Ghadeer varies by narration, the exact sequence at Karbala varies), pick the most widely accepted Shia version and note briefly: "I used the narration from [source] where [detail]. Let me know if you want a different version." Then proceed.
